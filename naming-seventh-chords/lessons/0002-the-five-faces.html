Lesson 2 · Naming seventh chords

The Five Faces

One skeleton — say C·E·G·B — can wear five different faces. Slide a note down a half-step and the same four letters become a brighter or darker chord with a different name. Those five faces are the five qualities, and they line up as one staircase: brightest at the top, darkest at the bottom, one lowered note per step.

Why this lesson, and why now

You came back from Lesson 1 fluent — naming the bottom note was instant, the treble staff reads clean.1 So we skip the staff warm-up entirely and go straight for the part you actually asked about: which seventh chords there are, and how to name them both ways.

Lesson 1 gave you the skeleton — the four letter-names. This lesson adds the accidentals (the sharps and flats) that turn a skeleton into a named chord. The letters tell you where; the accidentals tell you which.

1Quality = a triad + which seventh

You already read triads — major, minor, diminished — and a triad is just the bottom three notes of the snowman. A seventh chord adds the fourth note on top. So a seventh chord's quality is only ever two questions, and you already own the first:

1. What kind of triad is on the bottom?  (major / minor / diminished — your old skill.)
2. What kind of seventh sits on top?  (a major 7th, a minor 7th, or — rarely — a diminished 7th.)

That top interval is the only genuinely new thing here. A major seventh is the bright one — just a half-step under the octave, it leans hard up toward the root. A minor seventh sits a whole step under the octave — looser, bluesier. The diminished seventh is lower still, and shows up in exactly one chord.2

The letters never change — it's C·E·G·B in every card. Only the accidentals move. (That top note stays a B even in C°7: a double-flat 𝄫, so the stack is still B-on-the-line. Spell it A and you've broken the snowman.)

3The staircase: one note darker each step

This is the whole lesson in one picture. Start from the brightest chord and lower a single note at a time — the name changes at every rung.

Each step down is one key to the left on the piano — a half-step. Brightest (everything natural) at the top; darkest (everything squeezed down to stacked minor thirds) at the bottom. If you only remember one thing from this lesson, remember the staircase: maj7 → 7 → m7 → ø7 → °7, lowering one note at a time.

Why the symbols look the way they do. The plain number 7 means dominant — it's the most common seventh chord, so it got the bare name (a "C7" is never a Cmaj7). maj7 and m7 just name the triad underneath. ø7 is "half-diminished" because only the triad is diminished — the seventh is still an ordinary minor 7th. °7 is "fully-diminished" because the seventh got diminished too.1

The alias m7♭5 for half-diminished is worth knowing — it literally reads "minor-7th chord with a flatted 5th", which is exactly the recipe. You'll see it on lead sheets where you'd see ø7 in a textbook.

5The fourth-vs-fifth catch — the one you left out

When you set this up, you named four: major-7, minor-7, dominant-7, and "diminished-7". There are five — the missing one is the half-diminished (ø7), and it's not a footnote. Here's the catch worth pinning down, because "the diminished one" is really two different chords.

Take the all-white-key seventh chord built on B — B·D·F·A. No accidentals at all. What quality is it?

The white-key one, Bø7, is half-diminished — and it's the chord that sits naturally on the 7th note of the C-major scale, so it's the "diminished" you'll actually run into when you analyse a song in a major key.3 The fully-diminished B°7 needs that extra flat on the A; it's a chromatic guest (it comes from the minor scale, or from borrowing) — the tense passing chord you hear in jazz and older pop. Same letters, one accidental apart, two different names. Which one were you picturing? Worth telling me — it changes which examples I reach for next.

6Both directions, with the accidentals now

You asked to go both ways. The skeleton work from Lesson 1 is step one of each; the quality is just a second pass.

A shortcut for writing, once the staircase is in your ear: spell the maj7 first (the bright reference), then apply the flats the quality calls for — 7 is ♭7; m7 is ♭3 and ♭7; ø7 is ♭3 ♭5 ♭7; °7 is ♭3 ♭5 ♭♭7.

A handrail to the keyboard

The triad is still the chord you build in your sleep — so read it first and you're three-quarters done; only the seventh is new. Not sure of a quality? Play it. Major-7 sounds lush and resolved; dominant-7 sounds like it wants to move; minor-7 is mellow; the two diminished cousins sound tense and unfinished. Your ear already knows these from the songs you play — this lesson is just giving them names.

Stretch: the white-key seven

cliffhanger

You found that B·D·F·A (all white keys) is half-diminished. Now spell the all-white-key seventh chord on every other letter too: on C, D, E, F, G, A. Name each one's quality. You'll get a very particular pattern — two major-7ths, three minor-7ths, one dominant-7th, and that one half-diminished — and it is no accident.

Goal: see that the seven white-key seventh chords aren't random — they're the seventh chords of C major, and which quality lands where is the engine of how songs are built. That's Lesson 3.

I'm your teacher — ask me

Two things I'd like back. First: did the staircase land? The "lower one note per step" picture is the whole lesson — if it clicked, naming is now mostly the triad you already know plus one yes/no question about the seventh. Second, the open one from above: which "diminished" did you actually mean — the half (ø7, the white-key one that lives in major keys) or the full (°7, the tense chromatic one)? Both are real and both are here; knowing which you care about tells me whether Lesson 3 leans toward plain diatonic harmony or toward the chromatic colour chords. And as always — if any spelling or claim here looks off, challenge it; you've been right every time.
