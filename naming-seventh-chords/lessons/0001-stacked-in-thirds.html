Lesson 1 · Naming seventh chords

Stacked in Thirds

You build triads in your sleep. A seventh chord is one of those, with a single extra note stacked on top — and on the page it makes a shape so regular you can read it without really reading music: four notes climbing in thirds, a little snowman. Find the bottom note, skip up the alphabet, and the chord spells itself.

Why this lesson, and why now

Three workspaces about your hands; this one is about the page. You said the goal is to understand the harmony — to look at a chord and know its name, and to write a name back as notes.1 The catch you named: you read chord charts, not staves. So we don't "learn to sight-read" — we learn to decode one stacked chord, and it needs exactly one landmark, the same kind of anchor you already use to find keys in the dark.

Today is the skeleton only — the four letter-names. What makes a chord major-7 versus dominant-7 is a matter of sharps and flats, and that is Lesson 2. Walk before flesh.

1A seventh chord is a triad + one more third

Your triads are already stacks of thirds: take a root, skip a letter to land on the third, skip again for the fifth. G–B–D.1 A seventh chord just keeps going — one more skip, one more third on top. Skip up from D and you land on F:

“Skip a letter” is the move that builds everything here, and it shows up three ways at once. On the staff it’s every other line-or-space. In the alphabet it’s G–B–D–F (skip A, skip C, skip E). On your keyboard it’s every other white key:

2On the staff: the snowman

Because the notes climb in thirds, a seventh chord in root position can only make one of two pictures — four noteheads stacked tight, sitting all on lines, or all on spaces. Never a mix. That tidy four-high stack is the visual signature you're learning to spot — call it the snowman.

Both stacks fit entirely inside the five lines — handy first landmarks. The lowest note of the stack is always the root.

3Reading it: anchor, then skip

To name the stack you only have to read one note — the bottom one, the root. Everything above it is just skips, which you already know how to spell. And to read that one note, you need one landmark, exactly like finding C in the dark by the two-black-key group.

The landmark is the clef itself. The treble clef is a stylised letter G, and its curl wraps the second line from the bottom — so that line is G (the G just above middle C).2 From that one anchor, every line and space follows by skipping and stepping:

The whole reading procedure: (1) see a four-high stack → it's a seventh chord; (2) name the bottom note off the clef anchor — that's the root; (3) alphabet-skip up for the other three. Root C → C·E·G·B. Root D → D·F·A·C. Done.

4Both ways: read a stack, write a stack

You asked to go in both directions, and they're the same skip run forwards or backwards.

Read — staff to nameStepsWrite — name to staff
A stack sitting on the bottom space and up: F (space 1) … bottom note = root → skip up ×3 “Stack a seventh on F” → write a notehead on space 1, then every space above:
F · A · C · E ↔ F · A · C · E

To write, the snowman rule runs in reverse and keeps you honest: if the root sits on a space, all four notes are spaces; if on a line, all four are lines. Put the root down, then fill the next three same-kind slots going up.

A handrail to the keyboard

Not sure you read a notehead right? Check it against the keys you trust. Every stack on this page is white-key-only (all naturals, no sharps or flats yet), and on the keyboard a third is just every other white key — so F·A·C·E and the rest are easy to confirm under your hand. Staff says it, keys prove it.

5Trainer: name the stack · build the stack

Both directions, mixed. Read the snowman and pick its four letters — or, when asked, pick the stack that sits on a given root. Naming the bottom note fast is the whole game; the rest is skipping. Aim for a streak of ten.

6Take it to the page

The loop for this whole workspace: see it → say the letters → verify. Say them out loud; the saying is what makes the skip automatic.

spot the stack → name the bottom note → skip up: root · 3rd · 5th · 7th → verify on the keys

Read five snowmen

~3 min

From the trainer above (or print this page), read each stack aloud, bottom to top — “E… G, B, D”. Then find the four white keys and play them together. Does it sound like one solid chord? Then you read it right.

Goal: five stacks named correctly, each confirmed on the keyboard.

Write four from a name

~3 min

On paper or in your head, stack a seventh chord on each root: C, D, E, G, A. Say “root on a space → all spaces” or “root on a line → all lines”, then spell it: C·E·G·B, D·F·A·C, … Check each against the keys.

Goal: all five spelled without hesitating on the skip.

Close the loop automatically

~5 min

Run musictheory.net → Chord Identification and Chord Construction. It shows a chord on a staff and checks your answer instantly — both of your directions, automated.3 For now ignore the quality (maj/min/dom); just get the letters right. It's the tightest feedback loop there is for this.

Goal: ten chords identified, ten constructed — letters correct.

Stretch: the skeleton has a secret

cliffhanger

Write the snowman on C: C·E·G·B. Play it. Now play it again, but lower the B a half-step to B♭ — same skeleton, same four letters, but listen to how the colour changes. Lower the E to E♭ as well and it changes again. The letters didn't move; the quality did.

Goal: hear that one skeleton can wear different faces. Which faces — major-7, dominant-7, minor-7, and the two diminished cousins — and the single accidental that flips each one, is all of Lesson 2.

I'm your teacher — ask me

First thing I want to know: how was naming that bottom note? Instant, or did you count up from G every time? Your answer decides whether Lesson 2 jumps straight to qualities, or we spend ten minutes making the treble staff automatic first — no shame either way; you told me staves are new ground. Also: you listed four seventh chords, but there are five in common use (I'll show you the one you left out, the half-diminished — it's the chord hiding on the 7th note of every major scale).
