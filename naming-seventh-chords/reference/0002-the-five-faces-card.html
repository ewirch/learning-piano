Reference card · Naming seventh chords

The Five Faces

The skeleton (four letters) comes from Lesson 1. The quality is the accidentals on top of it. A quality is just a triad + which seventh — and there are five.

The bottom two thirds spell the triad: M·m = major, m·M = minor, m·m = diminished. The top third decides the seventh.

The staircase — lower one note per step

maj7→ 7♭7→ m7♭3→ ø7♭5→ °7♭♭7

Brightest → darkest, all on one skeleton (e.g. C·E·G·B). Each arrow drops one more note a half-step — one key to the left. To write fast: spell the maj7, then add the flats the staircase shows.

Both directions

Read — staff → name

  1. Spell the skeleton (bottom note, alphabet-skip).
  2. Name the triad (bottom three): major / minor / dim.
  3. Is the 7th major or minor? Combine → quality.

Write — name → staff

  1. Spell the skeleton from the root.
  2. Set the triad the name asks for (maj/min/dim).
  3. Add the seventh the name asks for.

Three traps

1. “7” alone = dominant. C7 is never a Cmaj7 — the bare number is the major-triad-plus-minor-7th chord.
2. Two diminisheds. ø7 = half (dim triad, ordinary minor 7th). °7 = full (the 7th is diminished too — a double-flat appears when you spell it in thirds).
3. m7♭5 means ø7 — same chord, lead-sheet spelling. It reads as its own recipe: minor-7th chord, flat the 5th.

The catch: the white-key “diminished”

The diatonic “diminished” chord in a major key is the half-diminished on degree 7. The fully-diminished needs an extra flat and comes from the minor side.

The whole card in one line: read the triad, then ask major or minor seventh? — that names it. Five faces on one skeleton, brightest to darkest: maj7 → 7 → m7 → ø7 → °7, one note lower each step.
