# Playing Chords Without Looking — Resources

## Knowledge

- [Playing Without Looking at One's Hands — Key-Notes](https://www.key-notes.com/blog/playing-without-looking-at-one-s-hands)
  Frames keyboard navigation as *proprioception* — the "sixth sense" of knowing
  where your body is in space — and explains finding octaves/notes by the feel
  of arm angle and finger span. Use for: the why behind eyes-closed practice.

- [Developing Keyboard Sense — eNovativePiano](https://onlinepianocurriculum.enovativepiano.com/developing-keyboard-sense/)
  Concrete drills: locate the 2- and 3-black-key groups with eyes closed; feel
  that "every major and minor key has a unique topography and therefore a unique
  feel under the hand"; block chords to feel their shape. Use for: drill design.

- [How To Play Piano Without Looking At The Keys — Playing Keys](https://playingkeys.com/play-piano-without-looking-at-the-keys/)
  Plain-language version of the black-key-group landmark rule (C = white key left
  of a 2-group, F = white key left of a 3-group). Use for: the anchor-note rule.

- [How to Master Piano Leaps Without Looking — Cooper Piano](https://cooperpiano.com/how-to-master-piano-leaps-without-looking/)
  On larger jumps and using physical landmarks + peripheral vision. Use later, if
  the mission grows beyond close-voiced accompaniment into wider leaps.

- [I–V–vi–IV progression — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/I%E2%80%93V%E2%80%93vi%E2%80%93IV_progression)
  The C–G–Am–F "four chords of pop": ubiquity, the Axis of Awesome "Four Chords"
  medley, and a long song list. Use for: grounding why this loop is worth drilling
  (lesson 2), and a source of real songs to build future lessons around.

- [What is voice leading? — Skoove](https://www.skoove.com/blog/voice-leading/)
  Plain-language: move each voice the smallest distance; the smoothest move is a
  common tone that doesn't move at all. Backed academically by
  [Common-Tone Chords — *Composing Music: From Theory to Practice*](https://rwu.pressbooks.pub/musictheory/chapter/root-position-connection-of-chords-in-the-major-mode/).
  Use for: the common-tone-as-glued-finger idea (lesson 2 onward).

- [How to Play Piano with Both Hands for Beginners — Hoffman Academy](https://www.hoffmanacademy.com/blog/how-to-play-with-both-hands-for-beginning-pianists)
  Get each hand solid and near-automatic *separately* first, then combine; expect
  hands-together to feel like starting over for a moment. Backs the Lesson-2 →
  Lesson-3 sequencing. See also
  [7 Ways to Improve "Hands Together" Coordination — Teach Piano Today](https://www.teachpianotoday.com/2016/07/13/7-ways-piano-students-can-improve-their-hands-together-coordination/).
  Use for: why we drilled hands separately before combining (lesson 3).

- [Great Left-Hand Accompaniment Patterns — dummies](https://www.dummies.com/article/academics-the-arts/music/instruments/piano/great-left-hand-accompaniment-patterns-for-the-piano-or-keyboard-153052/)
  The single root note per chord is the most basic left-hand pattern, the
  foundation the others (root+fifth, octaves, broken/stride bass) build on. Use
  for: justifying the single-root bass in lesson 3, and the menu of richer bass
  patterns to grow into later.

- [Sight Read Without Looking Down — Grand Piano Passion](https://grandpianopassion.com/sight-read-without-looking-down/)
  Why looking down breaks reading (continuity, read-ahead, concentration) and
  why players do it (insecurity — to verify); keeping eyes up starts as willed
  intention; legitimate glances are "only with the eyes, not the entire head".
  Also teaches finding common connections between hand positions — independent
  confirmation of our glued-finger handrail. Use for: the gaze discipline and
  planned-glance rule (lesson 4).

- [The 3 Secrets of Sight Reading — Robert Estrin, Living Pianos](https://livingpianos.com/the-3-secrets-of-sight-reading/)
  Read in larger groups to anticipate; "your ear will tell you when something's
  not right"; keep the flow, don't stop at mistakes. Use for: ear-as-verifier
  and the don't-stop rule (lesson 4 onward — central once real songs arrive).

- [How to Read Lead Sheets and Chord Charts — Ashley Danyew](https://www.ashleydanyew.com/posts/how-to-read-lead-sheets-and-chord-charts)
  Chord chart = chord symbols + lyrics, no notation, the standard accompaniment
  format (vs lead sheet = melody notated). Use for: the chart format taught in
  lesson 4, and terminology when real songs come in.
