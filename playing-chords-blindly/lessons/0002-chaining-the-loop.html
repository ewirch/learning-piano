Lesson 2 · Playing chords without looking

Round the Loop Without Looking

In Lesson 1 you changed between two chords blind by pivoting on the finger they share. Now we turn that one move into a navigation system, and use it to play the four-chord loop that hides under half of pop and folk — C – G – Am – F — all the way round, eyes closed, one hand.

Why this lesson, and why now

Last lesson ended with The Jump: change between two chords and lean on a finger that doesn't move — the handrail that stops you getting lost. That was the seed.

This lesson makes it systematic across a four-chord loop. At all but one change a single finger stays glued to its key right through the change — the handrail that keeps you oriented. Knowing where that finger is at every change — and spotting the one change that gives you none — is what lets you run the whole loop without a single glance at your hand.

1The idea: shared notes are your handrail

The four-chord loop C – G – Am – F (in Roman numerals, I – V – vi – IV) is the most-used chord pattern in modern pop — Let It Be, No Woman No Cry, With or Without You and hundreds more all ride on it; a comedy group famously medley'd dozens of hits over these same four chords to prove it.1 Learn to comp this loop blind and a huge slice of the songbook opens up.

The trick to moving between chords smoothly is older than pop. It's called voice leading, and its golden rule is laziness: when one chord changes to the next, every note should move the smallest possible distance. The smallest move of all is no move — a common tone, a note both chords contain, that simply stays where it is.2

For playing without looking, that lazy note is gold. When two chords share a note, the same finger can stay pressed on that key right through the change — a finger that never leaves the keys. That's your glued finger: the strongest orientation cue, the systematic version of Lesson 1's stationary finger. Your hand pivots around it while the other fingers move.

Three of this loop's four changes hand you a glued finger like that — one of them hands you two. The fourth shares no note at all: there the whole shape shifts by step, and the uniformity of that shift becomes its own cue. Spotting which is which at every change is the navigation skill — exactly the handrail from Lesson 1, now systematic across a whole loop.

One thing to notice first: every chord in this loop — C, G, Am, F — is built from white keys only. So here you cannot navigate by the black-vs-white tactile fingerprint from Lesson 1; all four shapes feel flat under the hand. Your orientation has to come from two other things: the glued common-tone finger, and your sense of the black-key groups sitting around your hand, telling you where on the keyboard your all-white shape is parked.

Why these fingerings — one rule. Your weakest fingers, 3 and 5, can't spread far: keep them within a third of each other. The thumb (1) is the stretchy one, so let it cover any fourth in the chord. When the fourth sits on the pinky side — out of the thumb's reach — slide the middle note onto finger 2 instead of 3, so 2–5 spans the fourth. That's the whole reason right-hand G and Am are 1·2·5 and left-hand F is 5·2·1: in each, the fourth lands next to the pinky. Everything else is a plain 1·3·5 / 5·3·1.

The whole progression lives in one hand-sized box. Three changes hand you a glued finger to ride — C → G (on G), Am → F (on both C and A), and F → C (on C). Only one — G → Am — shares nothing at all: every finger steps up exactly one white key in parallel, and that uniform motion is its own cue — if one finger drifts further than the others, you'll feel the shape go crooked.

3Train the navigation here

Before the piano, drill the map itself — for each change, which note do the two chords share? The first chord's notes are lit. 4Take it to the piano

Same feedback loop as Lesson 1 — and you run it yourself:

eyes closed → do the thing → open eyes to check → adjust → repeat

Do the whole sequence with your dominant hand first, then the other — both hands need this, and they learn it separately before they ever play together (that's next lesson).

Meet the four shapes

~2 min

Eyes open. Play each voicing in turn — C E G, B D G, C E A, C F A — and feel how each is a small box your hand barely has to reshape for. Then close your eyes: find C by feel (left of a 2-group) — it's the low note of three of the four shapes — and from there place each shape. Open to check.

Goal: form all four chords cold, and feel that they all sit in the same little patch of keyboard.

Ride the glued finger

~3 min

Two changes give you a single finger to ride. C → G: glue the finger on G (RH 5, LH 1). F → C: glue the finger on C (RH 1, LH 5). Eyes closed. Play the first chord, then change — but physically do not lift the glued finger; let it ride on its key while only the free fingers move one white key (C→G: the other two drop to B and D; F→C: the outer two drop to E and G). Open eyes, check. The finger that never left is the reason you didn't get lost.

Goal: each change with the glued finger staying pressed the whole way through, eyes closed.

Two handrails at once

~2 min

Am → F is the easiest change in the loop — two fingers stay glued. C (RH 1, LH 5) and A (RH 5, LH 1) both hold their keys; only the middle finger moves, E → F, one white key up. Eyes closed. Play Am (C E A), then change to F (C F A) without lifting either glued finger — the hand barely moves. Open eyes, check.

Goal: land F with both C and A held down the whole way, only the middle note moving, eyes closed.

The one with no handrail

~3 min

Now G → Am, the change that shares nothing. Eyes closed. Play G (B D G). Move every finger up exactly one white key, all the same distance, at the same moment → you land on Am (C E A). Your fingers don't change here — it stays 1·2·5 (right) / 5·3·1 (left), the whole shape just slides. If it feels lopsided, one finger overshot — reset and move them as one block. Open eyes, check. Then go back down (Am → G, everything down one).

Goal: make the no-common-tone move as one parallel block-shift, landing clean without looking.

Round the loop, blind

~5 min

The payoff. C → G → Am → F → C, eyes closed, one hand, slowly. As you make each change, say the rule out loud: “glue G… all up one… glue C and A… glue C.” Naming it keeps your hand honest. Open your eyes only at the end of each full loop to check, then close them and go again. Clean landings first — speed comes later.

Goal: four times round the loop, no looking mid-loop, landing each chord right. That's the lesson — a whole progression played blind.

5What “done” looks like — and what's next

When you can run the loop four times round with one hand, eyes closed, landing clean — you've done something real: you just comped a progression that's under hundreds of songs, without looking at the keys.

Next lesson we bring both hands together over this same loop — a simple bass note in the left hand under these shapes in the right — which is the last step before the real target: eyes on the lyric sheet, hands on their own. Keep these exact voicings; we'll build straight on top of them.
