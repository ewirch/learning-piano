Lesson 1 · Playing chords without looking

Finding Home Without Looking

Your eyes belong on the lyrics, the chord chart, or the people you're playing for — not on your hands. Before you can change chords blind, your fingers need to know where they are on their own. This lesson hands them a map they can read by touch.

Why this lesson, and why first

You already build chords and use inversions to keep your hands in one zone — that's exactly right, and it means your problem is not big leaps. Your problem is orientation: the instant you stop looking, your hands lose track of where they are.

So we fix orientation first. Everything you want next — smooth changes, both hands together, eyes on the page — is built on top of this one skill.

1The idea: a map you read with your fingers

The black keys aren't spaced evenly. They come in groups of 2 and 3, with a gap between each group. That irregular pattern is the whole trick — it's what lets you know where you are without looking, the same way a reader's fingertip finds its place among braille dots.1

From that pattern you get two anchor notes you can find anywhere on the keyboard, purely by feel:

C
is the white key just to the left of any group of 2 black keys.
F
is the white key just to the left of any group of 3 black keys.

The gaps matter too. Between E–F and between B–C there is no black key — your fingertip feels a wider seam of white. Those seams are landmarks in their own right, not just empty space.2

The point: you don't memorise 88 keys. You feel one repeating shape — 2 black keys, then 3 — and read everything else off those two anchors. This is called keyboard sense, and it's the same proprioception athletes and dancers rely on: your brain's sense of where your body is without watching it.1

3Take it to the piano

Now the part that actually rewires your hands. Every drill runs the same tiny feedback loop, and you run it yourself:

eyes closed → do the thing → open eyes to check → adjust → repeat

Do each drill with your left hand and your right hand separately — both need the map. The black-key landmarks work identically wherever your hand is.

Feel the 2s and 3s

~1 min

Eyes closed. Rest your fingertips lightly on top of the black keys — don't press. Slide slowly along and feel where two sit together, then three, then two, then three. Say it out loud: “two… three… two…”. Open your eyes and check you were right.

Goal: the 2–3 pattern becomes something you feel, not something you look at.

Land on home

~2 min

Eyes closed. Lift your hand right off the keys. Now find C: feel for a group of 2 and drop a finger on the white key just to its left. Play it. Open your eyes — did you land on C? Reset, and do the same for F (left of a group of 3). Then repeat, starting your hand from a different random spot each time.

Goal: find C and F cold, by feel, from anywhere — first try, most of the time.

Land one chord blind

~3 min

Pick one chord you know well, in a comfortable inversion — say C major. Eyes closed. Lift off. Find your anchor (C), then place your whole chord shape from there and play it. Before you open your eyes, notice the tactile fingerprint: which fingertips are resting on raised black keys, and which on flat white keys? That feeling is your built-in check. Now open your eyes and verify.

Goal: place a whole chord from an anchor, and learn to confirm it by feel before you ever look.

The jump

~3 min

The thing you came for. Choose two chords from a song you like — start with C and Am, then try C and F. Pick the inversion that keeps your hand in one place (you already do this), so the change is a small move and not a leap. Eyes closed. Play the first chord. Now change to the second by moving as little as possible: any finger on a note the two chords share stays glued down — that stationary finger is your anchor, the thing that stops you getting lost — and only the others slide, the shortest distance. Confirm the new fingerprint, then play. Move back. Slowly; open your eyes only to check, then close them again. Accuracy by feel first — speed comes later.

Goal: change between two chords without looking, pivoting on the finger that stays put. This is the skill — just in miniature.

Tactile fingerprint, defined: every chord shape has a unique feel — the pattern of which fingers sit on raised (black) keys versus flat (white) keys, and how far apart they sit. C major is all-white, flat under every finger; F major and D minor each have their own feel.2 Once you know a chord's fingerprint, you can confirm you've landed it correctly without a single glance.

4What “done” looks like — and what's next

When you can run The jump with two chords, each hand separately, landing right most of the time without looking — you've got the foundation. That's a real, tangible win: you just changed chords blind.

Next lesson we'll chain a full four-chord pop progression (the C–G–Am–F kind that's under half of folk and pop) and then bring both hands together over it — moving toward the real target: eyes on the lyric sheet, not the keys.
