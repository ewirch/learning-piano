Lesson 3 · Playing chords without looking

Both Hands, Eyes Closed

In Lesson 2 you ran the four-chord loop blind with one hand, then drilled the other on its own. Now we put them together: the right hand keeps playing those chords, and the left hand drops a single bass note underneath — C – G – Am – F, both hands, eyes off the keys. This is the real shape of accompaniment, and the last step before your eyes move to the lyric sheet.

Why this lesson, and why now

Last lesson you got each hand solid and separate on the loop — and that was on purpose. The reliable way to play hands together is to first know each hand well enough to run it without thinking, almost on autopilot; then the brain has room to coordinate the two.1 You're there.

So this lesson adds exactly one new thing: a left-hand bass note under the chords, and the skill of keeping both hands oriented at once without looking. Each hand already has its own handrail — the trick is trusting both at the same time.

1The idea: each hand keeps its own handrail

You already have a handrail in each hand — you built them in the last two lessons. Bringing the hands together doesn't replace them; it asks you to lean on both at once.

Right hand — the chord

Plays the same four shapes as Lesson 2: C E G · B D G · C E A · C F A. Its handrail is the glued finger — the shared note it rides through three of the four changes — and the uniform block-shift on the fourth. Nothing here changes.

Left hand — the bass

Plays one note: the root of each chord — C, G, A, F — about an octave below the right hand. A single root per chord is the foundational accompaniment pattern, the one every other left-hand style is built on.2 Its handrail is the black-key landmark from Lesson 1.

The whole new skill in one sentence: the right hand navigates by the glued finger; the left hand navigates by the black-key groups; and you hold both senses in mind at the same time, eyes closed. That's all Lesson 3 is — two handrails you already know, gripped together.

2The left-hand map: four roots by feel

The left hand only ever plays four notes — the roots C → G → A → F, looping back to C. The beautiful part: each one sits at a spot you can already find by touch, straight from Lesson 1. Two live at the left edge of a black-key group; the other two are tucked between the black keys of the three-group.

ChordBass rootHow it feels under the finger
CCWhite key just left of the 2-black group — your Lesson-1 anchor.
GGTucked between the first two keys of the 3-black group — raised keys on both sides.
AmATucked between the last two keys of the 3-black group — one white key up from G.
FFWhite key just left of the 3-black group — your other Lesson-1 anchor.

Play it with whatever left-hand finger feels steadiest landing a single note — the thumb (1) aims well; the pinky (5) gives a deeper, more planted bass. Pick one and keep it. The point isn't the finger — it's landing the note by the landmark, not by sight.

3How the hands fit together

Here's the move that makes both-hands-blind feel possible. Look at what each hand is doing at every change:

ChangeRight hand (the chord)Left hand (the bass)
C → Gglued finger on G — pivots, nearly stillleaps up a 5th to G
G → Amno handrail — shifts up one white keysteps up one to A
Am → Ftwo glued fingers (C & A) — barely movesdrops down a 3rd to F
F → Cglued finger on C — pivots, nearly stilldrops down a 4th to C
Let the still hand be your anchor. At three of the four changes — C→G, Am→F, F→C — your right hand barely moves: it pivots on a glued finger or holds two. So at those moments, treat the right hand as the fixed point and let the left hand measure its leap against it. Only one change, G→Am, asks both hands to travel — and there they travel the same way: both go up one white key, together. That shared little nudge upward is its own cue. So you're never asking both hands to make a big, independent move at the same time — there's always either a still hand to lean on, or a matching motion to ride.

4Train the bass map here

Before the piano, drill the left-hand map by feel. For each change, the keys are blank — no labels. Click the white key where the left-hand bass of the next chord lands, finding it by the black-key groups alone. You'll get the landmark, the bass move, and what the right hand is doing, every time.

5Take it to the piano

Same feedback loop as before — and it only works because you remove sight:

eyes closed → do the thing → open eyes to check → adjust → repeat

Expect bringing the hands together to feel, at first, like starting over — that's normal, and it's brief, because each hand already knows its part.1 Go slowly. Clean landings first; speed never.

The bass alone, blind

~2 min

Left hand only. Eyes closed. Find C (left of the 2-group), then play the bass loop: C → G → A → F → C, using the landmarks — G and A tucked in the 3-group, F at its left edge. Open your eyes at the end of each lap to check. Feel the shape of the journey: a leap up, a small step up, then settling back down.

Goal: the four roots landed by feel, eyes closed, without hunting for them.

One change, both hands, slow

~4 min

Now together — but one change at a time, and lean on the still hand. Start with the three that give you one: C→G (right hand glues G, left hand leaps up a 5th), Am→F (right hand holds C & A, left hand drops a 3rd), F→C (right hand glues C, left hand drops a 4th). Eyes closed: play the first chord-and-bass, then change — keep the right hand's glued finger pressed as your anchor while the left hand makes its move. Open eyes, check. Do each change a few times.

Goal: each of the three "still-hand" changes landed cleanly in both hands, eyes closed, with the right-hand anchor never lifting.

The one where both hands move

~2 min

G → Am is the only change with no still hand — so make the two hands agree. Eyes closed. Play G (chord B D G, bass G). Move everything up exactly one white key at the same instant: the right-hand shape block-shifts up one, the left-hand bass steps up one to A. Both hands rise together, the same distance. If it feels lopsided, one hand overshot — reset and move them as a single gesture. Open eyes, check.

Goal: G→Am as one unified up-a-step move in both hands, landing clean and level without looking.

Round the loop, both hands, blind

~5 min

The payoff. C → G → Am → F → C, both hands, eyes closed, slowly. Name the move as you go: "glue G, bass leaps up… both up one… glue C & A, bass drops… glue C, bass drops." Open your eyes only at the end of each full lap to check, then close and go again. When two or three laps land clean, try humming or saying the chord names aloud while you play — a first taste of doing something else while your hands run on their own.

Goal: four laps of the loop, both hands, no looking mid-lap, every chord and bass landing right. That's a complete two-handed accompaniment, played blind.

6What "done" looks like — and what's next

When you can run the loop a few times round with both hands, eyes closed, landing clean — you've built the actual engine of accompaniment: a bass and a chord, moving together, with your eyes free. Everything from here is about where those eyes go.

Next lesson is the mission itself: eyes on a lyric-and-chord sheet while your hands run this loop on their own. We'll build straight on top of these exact shapes and this exact bass — no new notes, just your gaze finally lifting off the keys and onto the page.
