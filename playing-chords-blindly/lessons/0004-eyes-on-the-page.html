Lesson 4 · Playing chords without looking

Eyes on the Page

Lesson 3 gave you the engine: chord and bass, both hands, eyes closed. This lesson is the mission itself — your eyes open onto a lyric-and-chord sheet while your hands run that same loop on their own. Same chords, same bass, not one new note. The only thing that moves is your gaze.

Why this lesson, and why now

You can run the loop blind with both hands — so the hands are done. What's untrained is attention: where your eyes rest, and what does the job of checking. Players look down out of insecurity — to verify a note just played or about to be played — and every look down breaks the reading, kills your chance to read ahead, and drags concentration to the keys.1

But verification is a job you've already re-assigned. Your hands confirm a landing by feel, and from today your ears confirm it by sound.2 Your eyes are free — and the page gets them.

1Nothing new in the hands — three rules for the eyes

Everything below the wrists is Lesson 3, untouched: right hand C E G · B D G · C E A · C F A, left hand bass roots C · G · A · F, the glued fingers, the landmarks, the still-hand anchor. What's new is a discipline for everything above the wrists:

1 · Eyes live on the page

Not "mostly". The gaze rests on the chart. Keeping your eyes up starts as a willed intention — deciding, before you play, that you will not look down to check.1

2 · Ears do the checking

With your eyes busy, sound is your feedback. A wrong landing announces itself instantly — your ear tells you when something's not right, and helps you adjust as you go.2

3 · Don't stop

A fumbled chord left behind beats a perfect chord that halts the song. Keep the flow; fix it next lap.2 When you're accompanying singing, the song doesn't wait for you.

The whole new skill in one sentence: your hands navigate by feel, your ears verify by sound, and your eyes do exactly one job — read the page. Three channels, no overlap. That's all accompaniment is.

2Your practice chart — and reading ahead

What singers and accompanists actually play from is a chord chart: lyrics with chord symbols written above them, no staff notation at all.3 Below is one written for this lesson. Every line is exactly one lap of your loop — C → G → Am → F — so your hands already know every note on the page. The chart isn't there to surprise them; it's there to give your eyes their new home. (As a bonus, verse two's lyrics are your own handrails — the chart teaches itself.)

And here's the one genuinely new reading skill: read ahead. Strong readers take the page in chunks, eyes ahead of the hands, so the next move is known before it's due.2 On a chord chart that's easy — the symbols are sparse. While your hands play one chord, your eyes drift to the next symbol. Eyes lead; hands follow.

3The glance economy

You're allowed to look down — on your terms. The difference is between a planned glance and a panic glance:

A planned glance happens at a break you chose in advance — the end of a line or verse — and it's made with the eyes only, head staying up, returning to the page within a beat.1 It re-anchors you without breaking the reading. A panic glance is the mid-change dive: head drops, reading stops, place lost. The drills below start by allowing a planned glance at every line break, then wean you to verse breaks, then to none. Panic glances are never on the menu — if a chord fumbles, your ears already told you, and the fix happens next lap.

4The moving chart

Put this page on your music stand (or print the chart). Press Start: after a count-in, the chart comes alive — the chord you should be playing lights solid, and the next one shades faint, pulling your eyes one symbol ahead, exactly the read-ahead habit from above. The click keeps you honest: it doesn't wait, so your hands learn to change on time and leave fumbles behind.

Slow is fine — 60 bpm, 4 beats per chord means one chord every four seconds.

The Looking-Up Song

key of C · every line is one lap: C → G → Am → F

Verse 1

CMy eyes were on my Gfingers, and my Amfingers knew the Fway,

Cbut the song lives on the Gpaper, and the Ampaper's far a-Fway.

CSo I'll trust the keys be-Gneath me, let my Amhands go on a-Flone,

Cwith my eyes upon the Gmusic, I can Amfind my way back Fhome.

Verse 2

CC lives by the Gtwo-group, and the AmF beside the Fthree,

CG and A sit Gtucked between the Amblack keys, snug and Ffree.

COne finger stays be-Ghind me, like a Amhandrail I can Fhold,

Cand when nothing stays, we Gboth go up to-Amgether, calm and Fbold.

Look at verse two's last line: "both go up together" changes chords mid-word — on purpose. That's the G→Am change, the one with no handrail, where both hands ride up one white key as a single gesture. The lyric is the cue.

5Take it to the piano

The feedback loop changes shape for the first time. Sight stays removed from the hands — but the check moves from your eyes to your ears, and the glance becomes a planned event, not a reflex:

eyes on the page → play → ears check every landing → planned glance at a break, only if needed → adjust next lap

Warm-up: eyes anywhere but down

~2 min

Both hands, the loop, eyes open — fixed on the wall above your music stand. No page yet. This sounds easier than eyes closed; many find it harder, because now there's something to resist: with eyes open, looking down is always one flick away. Hold the gaze on the wall and let the handrails do their job.

Goal: four clean laps, eyes open, gaze never dropping to the keys.

Follow the moving chart — hands only

~4 min

Chart on the stand, trainer at 60 bpm, 4 beats per chord, click on. No voice yet. Eyes track the lit chord and drift to the faint next one; hands change when the highlight does. If a chord fumbles, your ears caught it — keep going and fix it when the line comes around again. A planned glance (eyes only!) is allowed at each line break.

Goal: both verses in time with the click, no stops, no panic glances.

Say the words

~4 min

Same settings — now read the lyrics aloud, in rhythm, while your hands comp. Speaking is singing with the training wheels on: it occupies your voice and your reading attention the same way, without asking for pitch. You hummed through the loop at the end of Lesson 3; this is that, with the page added. Planned glances now only at verse breaks.

Goal: one full pass, voice never stopping, hands never stalling the click.

Sing it

~5 min

The mission. Make up any simple tune — or chant on one note if melody feels like too much at once; nobody's listening. Sing both verses while both hands comp and your eyes stay on the page. Nudge the tempo wherever it feels good. When verse two's last line lands — "both go up together" — enjoy that your hands do exactly what you're singing.

Goal: both verses sung, both hands comping, eyes on the page start to finish — zero glances is the prize, but two planned ones still count as a win.

6What "done" looks like — and what's next

Read your mission statement again: play a pop/folk progression with both hands while looking at the page, sing while comping, eyes off the keys. The last drill is that, end to end. When it lands, you haven't finished a lesson — you've done the thing you came here to do, in its first complete form.

From here the mission grows outward, and you get to pick the direction: a real song next (hundreds of pop and folk songs run on exactly these four chords4 — name one you actually want to sing); a richer left hand (root-and-fifth, octaves, broken bass — the patterns flagged at the end of Lesson 3); or another key, where the black keys finally join the chords and your tactile fingerprints earn their keep.

I'm your teacher — ask me

Tell me how it went. Does the gaze keep dropping at one particular change (my bet: G→Am, the no-handrail one)? Does the singing fall apart the moment the hands move, or the hands the moment you sing?
