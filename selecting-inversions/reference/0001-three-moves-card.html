The Three Moves

Texture: right hand close triad + left hand bass root (keyboard style, four voices). Count the root interval, apply the move — the inversion chooses itself.

⚠ One exception clause: V→vi (G→Am in C) classifies as mirror but carries an extra rule — leading tone up, doubled 3rd. Lesson 2.

The rulebook being respected

Forbidden

Parallel 5ths and parallel octaves between any two voices — bass included; compounds (12ths) count.

Fine

Parallel 3rds, 4ths and 6ths. Oblique motion (one voice holds) is always safe — that's what holding is.

Free of charge

Root in bass + full triad in RH = root doubled automatically. Leading tone in a V chord walks up to the tonic.

Second pass — the fingers

The move decides the keys; the fingering rule decides the fingers: 3-and-5 within a third · thumb takes any fourth · pinky-side fourth puts the middle note on finger 2 (= the standard taught fingerings: RH 1·3·5 / 1·2·5 / 1·3·5, LH 5·3·1 / 5·3·1 / 5·2·1 by position*). A held key keeps its finger only if the fingering pattern survives the change; RH patterns flip entering/leaving 1st-inversion shapes — a held middle key then re-fingers 3↔2 underneath. LH triads mirror this (patterns flip at 2nd-inversion shapes; from root position, falling roots keep both fingers) — academic while the LH plays single bass roots. Hold-one changes obey the same pass; mirror changes hold nothing, so nothing can swap.

The loop closes on the exact starting C·E·G. C→Am is the loop's only held-key finger swap — the falling-3rd-from-root-position cell of the grid above.

The mantra: hold what's shared, step what isn't — and when nothing is shared, mirror the bass. Keys first, fingers second: a held key earns a glued finger only if the fingering pattern survives. The lazy same-direction slide at a 2nd-apart change is where parallel octaves and fifths live. The chord is never wrong; the road is.

Terminology note: while the left hand plays roots, the harmony is always root position — the RH "inversion" you select is really the rotation of the three upper voices. Textbook names for the moves: common-tone & stepwise / contrary-motion & nearest (MT21C 26.7).
