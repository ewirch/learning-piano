Lesson 1 · Selecting inversions

The Three Moves

You already play smooth inversions — when someone hands them to you. This lesson hands you the machine instead: read the distance between two roots off the chart, apply the matching move, and the next chord's shape falls out — rulebook-correct, and already under your hand. There are no wrong inversions. There are only wrong roads between them.

Why this lesson, and why now

In the blind-play workspace you caught the parallel fifths at G→Am yourself — but the fix was derived for you. Detection you have; generation is the missing piece. The good news: in your texture — right hand close triad, left hand bass root, four voices in total — the classic part-writing rulebook compresses into three mechanical moves, chosen by one number you can read straight off any chord chart.1

1The frame: you already play four-voice harmony

Three voices in the right hand, one in the left — that's keyboard style, the piano layout of four-part writing. The rulebook you asked to respect says, in full:2 no two voices — bass included — may move in parallel fifths or parallel octaves (compounds like twelfths count too); parallel 3rds, 4ths and 6ths are fine. And one freebie: with the root in the bass and a complete triad in the right hand, the preferred doubling — doubled root — happens by itself.1 Your texture is rule-legal by construction; only the roads between chords can break it.

Which inversion is it, really?

While your left hand plays roots, every chord is technically in root position — the bass note decides that, not the right hand. What you're selecting is the rotation of the three upper voices. We'll keep calling these RH shapes "inversions", as you do; just know that the day the bass starts inverting (C/E, G/B — a later lesson), the word gets its strict meaning back.

2The three moves

Everything turns on the root interval — the letter distance between consecutive roots on the chart. It alone fixes how many keys the two chords share. Whether a shared key also keeps its finger is a separate, second decision — your own rule answers it, in the fingers pass below. Three cases, three moves:1

Roots are…Shared keysThe moveExample

Second pass: the fingers

The move decides the keys; your own rule decides the fingers — 3-and-5 within a third, thumb takes any fourth, pinky-side fourth puts the middle on 2. That rule is no private habit: it generates exactly the standard taught fingerings for triads and inversions (RH 1·3·5 / 1·2·5 / 1·3·5, LH 5·3·1 / 5·3·1 / 5·2·1).5 The consequence you discovered in the old workspace now becomes a rule of its own: a held key keeps its finger only if the fingering pattern survives the change. For the right hand the pattern flips whenever the change enters or leaves a 1st-inversion shape — and if the middle note is one of the held keys, the swap happens under it (3↔2, your "held note, re-fingered"). For hold-two changes, the full right-hand grid:

RH starts from…root rises a 3rd (C→Em)root falls a 3rd (C→Am)
root positionboth fingers gluemiddle held, 3→2

2nd inversionboth fingers glueboth fingers glue

Left-hand triads mirror this exactly (the pattern flips at 2nd-inversion shapes, so from root position it's falling roots that keep both fingers) — academic for now, since your left hand plays single bass roots. The same second pass governs hold-one changes too: the one shared key occasionally sits in a flipping middle and re-fingers. Mirror changes hold nothing, so there's nothing to lose.

Reading it off the chart, at speed: neighbour letters (F–G, G–A, C–D) → mirror. Letters with exactly one between (C–A, A–F, E–C) → hold two. Everything else (C–F, G–C, D–G) → hold one. You never compute the shared notes — the letter distance already did.

Notice what just happened to the word "selecting": you don't audition three inversions and pick one. You apply the move, and the inversion chooses itself — always the one nearest to your hand. Correctness and economy of motion come from the move; comfort comes from the fingers pass. Two decisions, both mechanical, no taste required.

3Why the mirror — the trap where the rules earn their keep

Hold-two and hold-one barely can go wrong: held keys pin the hand, the rest creeps stepwise. The danger lives entirely where roots are a 2nd apart and nothing is shared. There the lazy instinct — slide the whole hand up with the bass — manufactures parallels against the bass wholesale:2

Legal isn't the same as nearest

The safety here comes from one fact — the hand moving opposite the bass, so no upper voice can run parallel with it. But "opposite the bass" doesn't name a single chord. Keep falling past B·D·G to a root-position G — G3·B3·D4 — and it's still legal: contrary motion, no parallel 5ths or 8ves, not one voice leaping a 7th. What rules it out isn't the rulebook; it's nearest chord tone. B·D·G shifts each finger a step or a third — six semitones of travel all told; the root-position G leaps every finger a 4th or 5th — eighteen — to land the very same notes. No-parallels keeps you legal; nearest is what makes the shape unique, and what keeps the lap below closing on the exact C·E·G it started from.

Check the seams: every change obeys its move, the leading tone resolves at G→C, the root stays doubled the whole lap, and after four changes you land on the identical C·E·G you started from — the loop closes by itself. The bass walks C3·A2·F2·G2, every move a 4th or smaller, found by the same black-key landmarks as ever.

6Take it to the piano

The feedback loop of this whole workspace: classify before you touch the keys — then let your hands confirm what your mouth predicted.

say the move → predict what glues, what holds-and-swaps, what steps → play the change → verify: did the prediction land?

Call the move

~3 min

Both hands, the '50s loop, slow. Before every change say the classification aloud — "third apart, hold two" … "second apart, mirror" — then make the change. The saying is the drill; don't skip it once it feels obvious. That voice is what you'll hear in your head at playing speed.

Goal: two laps with the correct call before every change, no pauses to think.

Glue check

~3 min

Same loop, but now run the second pass out loud: before each change sort every held key into glued ("thumb stays put") or held-and-swapped ("E holds, 3 hands it to 2") — then play it and check whether the sorting proves true under your hand. C→Am is the loop's only swap; everything else glues for real.

Goal: one lap where every finger-prediction proves true under your hand.

Hear the trap

~2 min

At F→G only, with the bass: first play the lazy road on purpose — slide everything up to D·G·B over G2 — and listen. Then play the mirror — hand falls to B·D·G as the bass rises. The lazy road sounds like one thick block marching; the mirror sounds like four people walking. This is what parallel octaves and fifths are: voices giving up their independence.2 Once heard, your ear joins the rulebook's side.

Goal: play both roads twice and describe the difference out loud in your own words.

Stretch: derive your old loop — and hit the wall

cliffhanger

Run the three moves over the old blind loop, C–G–Am–F, from the same home C. Three changes fall out instantly and reproduce your drilled shapes to the finger: C→G (hold one), Am→F (hold two), F→C (hold one). Then try G→Am. It classifies as mirror — but play what the mirror produces over the bass and check it against the rules. Something refuses. (Hint: your current blind loop's G→Am, the block-shift you've drilled, also has a secret — check the bass against the right hand's G and D.)

Goal: not to solve it — to see precisely where it breaks. Bring me your findings. V→vi is the one change with an exception clause,1 and it's the whole of Lesson 2.
